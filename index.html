About

I am currently a Research Assistant Professor in the Department of Physics at Clarkson University in Potsdam, New York. My background includes a Ph.D. and M.S. in Physics from Clarkson University, and a B.A. in Physics from SUNY Plattsburgh, with additional coursework in mathematics, and chemistry.

My research centers on statistical modeling, time series analysis, anomaly detection, and information-theoretic inference, with applications to networked systems. I enjoy building models that not only fit data, but also provide interpretable insight and actionable predictions.

Beyond research, I am deeply engaged in teaching and mentoring as well as public outreach.

Research

My research focuses on the statistical structure and dynamics of complex systems, with an emphasis on time series, anomaly detection, and network modeling. I work at the intersection of physics, statistics, and machine learning, often using information-theoretic tools to extract structure from noisy, high-dimensional data.

Areas of interest

  • Statistical modeling: Probabilistic models for complex, noisy, and partially observed systems.
  • Time series & anomaly detection: Methods for identifying regime shifts, failures, and rare events in streams of data.
  • Network modeling: Inference of connectivity, dependencies, and interaction structure from multivariate observations.
  • Information-theoretic inference: Using entropy, mutual information, and related quantities to detect structure and causality.
  • Applied machine learning: Algorithm design that balances predictive performance, interpretability, and robustness.

Selected directions and projects

  • System health and resilience: Developing indicators and models that track system health over time, detect early warning signals, and guide intervention.
  • Data-driven models for complex networks: Learning network structure and dynamics from empirical data, with emphasis on uncertainty quantification.
  • Anomaly detection in high-dimensional systems: Building tools that can distinguish meaningful deviations from normal variability in multivariate signals.
  • Algorithmic frameworks for real-world constraints: Bridging theory and practice by integrating domain knowledge, limited data, and deployment constraints.

Teaching

My teaching spans core physics, mathematical methods, and data-oriented courses. I aim to connect rigorous theory with intuition, visualization, and real-world applications, helping students build both conceptual understanding and technical fluency.

Teaching philosophy

  • Conceptual clarity: Emphasizing deep understanding of underlying principles, not just procedural skill.
  • Multiple representations: Integrating equations, diagrams, simulations, and physical intuition to reinforce ideas.
  • Problem-solving as a craft: Guiding students through the full problem-solving process: modeling, approximation, computation, and interpretation.
  • Bridging theory and practice: Highlighting connections to data analysis, modeling, and modern computational tools.

Courses and roles

  • Postdoctoral / Research Associate roles: Mentoring students and collaborators on time series analysis, anomaly detection, and algorithm development.
